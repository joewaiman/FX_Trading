## Per-Currency Detail

**USD — Strong Long (7.5/9)**
CPI at 3.3% YoY keeps the Fed on hold at 3.63% (mid-range), with only one cut now penciled for late 2026 — a hawkish shift from December projections. Services ISM at 54.0 marks 21 consecutive months of expansion; manufacturing PMI at 52.3 is accelerating. Unemployment fell to 4.3% in March with 2.2% GDP growth forecast for 2026. The only negatives are a widening trade deficit (shipping/tariff headwinds) and rate expectations that technically lean dovish (one cut priced). Overall, the economy's breadth of strength supports the dollar near-term.

**AUD — Strong Long (7.0/9)**
The RBA has hiked twice in 2026 (Feb and Mar) to 4.10%, with inflation at 3.7% still above the 2–3% band — further hike risk remains. Inflation expectations have surged to 5.9% (highest since Nov 2022), driven by Middle East energy prices. GDP grew 0.8% in Q4 2025 (est. ~3.2% annualised) and unemployment is a healthy 4.3%. The key risk is PMI weakness: both manufacturing (49.8) and services (46.3) have contracted sharply, and the composite fell to 46.6 in April — a potential early warning for slowing domestic demand. The rate bias is the dominant driver here.

**GBP — Mild Long (6.0/9)**
CPI at 3.0% is 1pp above target and the BoE now expects it to rise to 3.0–3.5% in H2 2026 due to energy passthrough. 90% of economists expect a hold on April 30, with 10% pricing a hike — a hawkish shift from the two cuts that were expected pre-Middle East conflict. Manufacturing PMI held at 51.0 and services at 50.5, though both are weakening. Unemployment at 5.2% is drifting higher. The trade deficit is narrowing (£6bn vs £12.5bn). Overall: defensively bullish — the inflation backdrop prevents cuts, but growth headwinds limit conviction.

**JPY — Mild Long (6.0/9) — carry caveat**
Domestic fundamentals are deceptively solid: services PMI at 53.4, manufacturing at 51.4, unemployment at 2.6% (30-year low). However, CPI fell to 1.3% in February — below the BoJ's 2% target for the first time since 2022. This directly undermines the case for an April hike; Governor Ueda has avoided signalling one. The BoJ wants to hike but CPI doesn't support urgency. Until CPI returns above 2%, the hike timeline extends and JPY strengthening is deferred. Rate at 0.75% vs USD at 3.63% = 2.75% carry disadvantage. Score reflects domestic economic health, not the FX rate outlook.

**EUR — Mild Long (5.0/9)**
The ECB is on hold at 2.0% (deposit rate) and is expected to stay there through at least mid-2026. Inflation revised up to 2.6% (March) — driven by energy (up 5.1%), not core strength. Unemployment edged up to 6.2%, a negative. Composite PMI dropped to 50.7 in March (from 51.9) with services stagnating at 50.2 — growth momentum is losing steam. Trade surplus narrowed sharply (€11.5bn vs €23.1bn year prior). ECB hold + elevated unemployment + fading growth = limited upside. Long EUR only as the better of two weak options.

**CHF — Mild Long (5.0/9) — structural, not cyclical**
CHF's score is supported by a record trade surplus (CHF 4.4bn Feb, largest since Aug 2025) and very low unemployment (3.1%). However, the underlying economy is weak: GDP forecast at 0.9% for 2026, manufacturing PMI contracting at 47.4, and CPI at just 0.1% — at the floor of the SNB's 0–2% target band with rates already at 0%. The SNB has no room to cut and no reason to hike until 2027. CHF is fundamentally supported by its current account, not by rate dynamics. Watch for SNB intervention risk if CHF strengthens excessively on safe-haven flows.

**NZD — Neutral (4.5/9)**
The RBNZ held at 2.25% in April with CPI at 3.1% (just above the 1–3% band) — technically hawkish, but the committee is "not close to hiking." Services PMI has contracted for two consecutive months (46.0 in March), retail spending was weak in early 2026, and unemployment remains elevated at 5.4%. Manufacturing is a bright spot at 53.2. The picture is genuinely mixed: inflation above target but economy too fragile to tighten. Avoid NZD as a base or quote in high-conviction pairs.

**CAD — Strong Short (2.0/9)**
CAD is the clearest short in the basket. GDP contracted 0.6% in Q4 2025, unemployment is at 6.7% (highest in years), and the merchandise trade deficit widened to $5.7bn in February. The BoC is on pause at 2.25% after an aggressive easing cycle from ~5%, but with growth risks tilted firmly downward, further cuts are plausible. Manufacturing PMI stagnated at 50.0. CPI bounced to 2.4% in March (from 1.8%) on energy — the only thing preventing an immediate cut. CAD offers a compelling short against strong-bias currencies.
