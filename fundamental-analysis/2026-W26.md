## Macro Context

The defining backdrop for W26 is the **Iran–US war energy shock**. Global oil and energy prices spiked earlier in 2026 and remain elevated following the Middle East conflict, forcing central banks that had been cutting rates to pause, hold, or even reverse course. A US–Iran peace deal was recently signed, reducing (but not eliminating) tail risks. Key consequences:

- **Fed** (FOMC June 17): Held at 3.50–3.75% for 4th meeting; dot plot now skewed hawkish — 9 of 19 members see at least one hike in 2026, likely October.
- **ECB** (June 11): **Hiked** 25bp to 2.40% to prevent war-driven energy inflation from becoming entrenched.
- **BoJ** (June): **Hiked** 25bp to 1.0% — highest since 1995 — citing energy shock inflation risks.
- **BoE** (June 17): Held at 3.75%, split 7–2 with hawks wanting a hike. Market pricing rate increase possible.
- **SNB** (June 18): Held at 0% — expected at zero through end-2026.
- **MAS** (April): **Increased** S$NEER slope (tightened) — hawkish shift.
- **RBNZ**: Held at 2.25% (3rd hold); cutting cycle complete, next move UP.
- **BoC**: Held at 2.25% (5th hold); no cuts or hikes expected through 2026.
- **RBA**: Held at 4.35% in June; Westpac expects August hike; no cuts in 2026.

## Per-Currency Detail (Non-Neutral Biases)

### 🟢 SGD — Mild Bullish (strongest signal)
Singapore's 6% YoY GDP growth is the standout in the developed world. MAS tightened its S$NEER slope in April 2026 — a hawkish move in response to imported energy inflation — marking a shift from the January hold. The composite PMI of 56.7 and manufacturing PMI of 51 confirm broad economic expansion. The trade surplus of SGD 5.6B and near-zero inflation (1.8%) give MAS room to tighten further without damaging growth. Unemployment at 2% is at the overheating boundary, but reflects genuine full employment rather than distress. Key risk: SGD/USD is the natural expression but MAS manages the NEER, meaning FX moves may be moderated by policy.

### 🟢 USD — Mild Bullish
The Fed held for the 4th consecutive meeting but the dot plot shifted decisively hawkish — 9 of 19 participants now expect at least one hike in 2026, with October as the likely timing. CPI at 4.2% (May 2026) is well above the 2% target and elevated energy prices from the Middle East conflict continue to pressure prices. Manufacturing PMI at 55.7 is the strongest in the G8. Unemployment at 4.3% is within the healthy band. The drag is from negative real wages (-0.7% YoY) and the persistent trade deficit (-$55.9B). Overall: a currency with a hawkish Fed backing it, strong Mfg sector, and elevated inflation — net bullish but not overwhelmingly so.

### 🟢 INR — Mild Bullish (⚠️ liquidity caveat)
India's fundamentals are among the strongest in the 19-currency universe. GDP 7.8% YoY, Manufacturing PMI 54.5, Services PMI 57.3 — all reflecting an economy firing on multiple cylinders. CPI at 3.93% is near the midpoint of the 2–6% band, suggesting no policy emergency. The RBI is holding at 5.25% (positive real rate). The only significant red flag is the -$28.2B trade deficit. Important caveat: INR is a managed/NDF currency — USD/INR is the main instrument but liquidity and hedging costs are higher than G8 pairs. Use as a conviction indicator, not necessarily a primary trade.

### 🟢 NOK — Mild Bullish
Norway sits in a strong position: CPI 3.1% above target, Norges Bank holding at 4.25% (hawkish), unemployment at 4.4% (in band), and a massive trade surplus of NOK 62.6B driven by oil exports. The Iran war energy shock is a direct tailwind for NOK — higher oil prices improve Norway's terms of trade. GDP growth is moderate (1.7% YoY, 0.4% QoQ). PMI data is unavailable since the DNB PMI series was discontinued in December 2025 — a data gap to note. The high rate differential vs EUR and SEK makes NOK attractive.

### 🟢 BRL — Mild Bullish (⚠️ EM carry risk)
Brazil's SELIC rate of 14.25% makes it the highest-yielding liquid EM currency and a carry trade vehicle. CPI at 4.72% is above the 1.5–4.5% target band, and the Banco Central do Brasil is holding rates high to combat persistent inflation. The unemployment rate of 5.8% (below the 8% neutral band = bullish) and trade surplus of $7.8B provide additional support. Negatives: manufacturing PMI 49.1 (slight contraction) and retail sales -1.5% MoM suggest consumer stress. BRL is suitable only for traders comfortable with EM risk — wide spreads, sudden drawdowns, and political volatility can override fundamentals.

### 🟢 AUD — Mild Bullish (weakening)
CPI at 4.0% is above the RBA's 2–3% target band, and the RBA held at 4.35% in June — a hawkish hold. Westpac expects a hike in August. GDP slowed sharply to 0.3% QoQ (vs 0.9% prior), unemployment is 4.5% (at the top of the healthy band), and services PMI dipped to 49.9 (marginal contraction). The trade surplus of AUD 1.8B and elevated rate support AUD. But momentum is deteriorating — the bullish case is predicated on the RBA either holding or hiking; any cut signals would flip this to Neutral.

### 🔴 EUR — Mild Bearish (primary short candidate)
The euro faces a stagflationary trap. GDP contracted -0.2% QoQ in Q1 2026 (YoY just +0.3%), the weakest performance among G8 economies. Despite the ECB hiking rates to 2.4% in June (to prevent war-driven energy inflation from becoming entrenched), the underlying growth picture is weak: services PMI 48.9 (contraction), retail sales -0.4% MoM, and unemployment 6.3% (above the bearish threshold of 5.5%). The ECB's own projections now see only 0.8% GDP growth for 2026. The rate hike is forced and likely unsustainable — markets will eventually price in a reversal if growth continues to contract. ECB is hiking against a contracting economy: this is a stagflation signal, not a bullish one.

### 🔴 SEK — Mild Bearish
Sweden is caught between above-target PMIs (manufacturing 57.3, services 53.9 — surprisingly strong) and deteriorating macro fundamentals. CPI/CPIF at 0.8% is well below the 2% Riksbank target. GDP contracted -0.2% QoQ. Unemployment at 9.4% is above the 7–8.5% healthy band for SEK. The Riksbank is on hold at 1.75% and raised the probability of a hike later in 2026, but with CPI so far below target, this feels like a hawkish tilt driven by external energy risks rather than genuine domestic inflation. The trade deficit and sub-target CPI are the primary bearish signals. Positive PMIs prevent this from being a Strong Bearish call.

### 🔴 HUF — Mild Bearish
Hungary's most bearish signal: CPI at 1.8% is below the lower bound of the 2–4% target band. With the policy rate at 6.0% — the highest in the CEE region — and inflation falling below target, the National Bank of Hungary will face pressure to cut rates. Retail sales -1.2% MoM shows consumer weakness. PMIs are borderline (50.2 manufacturing, services N/A). GDP growth of 1.7% YoY is modest. The combination of below-target inflation + elevated rate = an inevitable cutting cycle that will pressure HUF lower. Preferred expression: LONG USD/HUF or LONG EUR/HUF.

### 🔴 ILS — Mild Bearish (⚠️ conflict risk)
The Bank of Israel cut from 4.0% to 3.75% — an easing bias. GDP contracted -0.8% QoQ (March 2026), clearly impacted by the ongoing Middle East conflict and military operations. Trade deficit at -$3.7B. While retail sales surprised at +3.3% MoM and unemployment is low (2.8%), the geopolitical overhang makes ILS fundamentally bearish. PMI data unavailable (⚠️ data-limited). Any escalation in conflict materially worsens the picture. Prefer to avoid ILS trades unless the conflict risk is explicitly priced in and a clear catalyst is available.

### 🔴 CNY — Mild Bearish (⚠️ PBOC-managed)
China's CPI at 1.2% represents genuine deflation risk — well below the informal ~3% target. Retail sales declined -0.38% MoM (and -0.6% YoY), signaling weak domestic demand. The PBOC's LPR rate at 3.5% reflects an easing bias, and further cuts are likely to stimulate growth. The GDP at 5.0% YoY looks solid in isolation, but the deflationary dynamics and declining consumption are structural concerns. The massive trade surplus of $105B is the one bright spot. Critical caveat: **CNY is PBOC-managed** — policy discretion (e.g., daily fixing adjustments) can override fundamental signals at any time. Use CNH (offshore) if trading, and remain aware that policy interventions can create sudden moves.
