## 1. Economic Highlights

### USD — United States Dollar
April non-farm payrolls surprised sharply to the upside, adding **115,000 jobs** (vs. 55,000 forecast), while the unemployment rate held steady at **4.3%**; average hourly earnings rose 0.2% MoM (+3.6% YoY), slightly below expectations — a Goldilocks combination of strong hiring and softer wage inflation. February payrolls were revised down 23K to −156K, though March was revised up 7K to +185K. Markets rallied on the print, with the S&P 500 extending its sixth consecutive winning week.

### EUR — Euro
Eurozone flash CPI for April accelerated to **3.0% YoY** (from 2.6% in March), the highest since September 2023, driven by energy costs surging **+10.9%** on Middle East supply disruptions — the largest energy spike since February 2023. The Eurozone Manufacturing PMI held steady at 52.2 (signalling solid expansion) and Services PMI rose to 52.6, suggesting broad economic resilience despite energy headwinds. ECB staff had previously forecast 2026 average inflation at 2.7%; April's print significantly exceeds that path.

### GBP — British Pound
The Bank of England held Bank Rate at **3.75%** at its April 29 meeting (8–1 vote). UK CPI stood at 3.3% YoY in March; the MPC indicated inflation is likely to stay between 3.0–3.5% in Q2-Q3 2026 due to elevated energy prices. Monthly GDP grew 0.5% (Dec 2025–Feb 2026 rolling period). The unemployment rate rose to 4.9% (332K new jobs added in the year to Feb 2026). The next MPC decision is June 18.

### JPY — Japanese Yen
The Bank of Japan kept its policy rate at **0.75%** at its April 28 meeting (6–3 split vote, with dissenters calling for a hike to 1.0%), while raising its core CPI forecast to **2.8%** (from 1.9%) and cutting its FY2026 GDP growth forecast to **0.5%** (from 1.0%) — citing Middle East-driven energy supply risks. JPY was the week's top G10 performer (+2.4% vs USD) as the hawkish dissent signalled an eventual policy shift; the Nikkei 225 broke above **62,000 for the first time** on May 7.

### AUD — Australian Dollar
The Reserve Bank of Australia raised the cash rate by **25 bp to 4.35%** at its May meeting (8–1 board vote) — the **third consecutive hike** this year — citing persistent above-target inflation compounded by Middle East-driven fuel prices. CBA economists expect a pause for the remainder of 2026; Westpac forecasts two additional hikes to 4.85%. AUD was little changed on net as the rate hike had been partially priced in.

### NZD — New Zealand Dollar
The RBNZ held the Official Cash Rate at **2.25%** at its latest review. New Zealand's Q1 2026 labour market statistics were released this week, showing continued employment resilience. NZD was a joint-top G10 outperformer, appreciating **+2.30% vs USD**, supported by improved global risk sentiment and strong China PMI data boosting demand for antipodean risk.

### CAD — Canadian Dollar
Canada's core CPI (average of trim and median measures) slowed to **2.8% annual rate** in April (from 3.1% in March) — the fourth consecutive month of moderation, suggesting BoC rate hikes are effectively cooling domestic demand. The CAD modestly underperformed, weakening ~0.28% vs USD as markets trimmed bets on early BoC rate cuts but found little impetus to buy the loonie aggressively.

### CHF — Swiss Franc
No major Swiss economic releases this week. The CHF gained modestly (~+0.3% vs USD) from its safe-haven status amid ongoing geopolitical uncertainty and Middle East tensions. The SNB's next scheduled policy assessment is June 2026; the SNB policy rate remains at 0%.

### CNY — Chinese Yuan Renminbi
Caixin Manufacturing PMI for April climbed to **52.2** (prior: 50.8; forecast: 51.0), the fastest factory expansion since December 2020, with new orders surging at the strongest pace in 14 months. Caixin Services PMI rose to **52.6** (prior: 52.1), pointing to broad-based economic momentum. The PBoC maintained its USD/CNY fixing around 6.80–6.83, allowing modest CNY appreciation reflecting improved fundamentals.

### INR — Indian Rupee
HSBC India Manufacturing PMI rose to **55.9** in April (from 53.9 in March), the strongest reading since early 2025, signalling a marked acceleration in factory output. HSBC Services PMI also firmed. The INR traded near 94.32 vs USD — edging slightly weaker — as elevated Brent crude above $110/bbl continued to weigh on India's large energy import bill, widening the current account deficit.

### BRL — Brazilian Real
COPOM cut the Selic rate by **25 bp to 14.50%** on April 29, then on May 5 released a **hawkish statement**, raising its 2026 inflation view to **4.6%** and warning that sustained Middle East-driven energy prices may force a slowdown or pause in the easing cycle. Despite the pivot, the BRL strengthened ~1.8% vs USD (USDBRL near 4.89 — the weakest USD level since January 2024); BRL is up **~10.5% vs USD year-to-date**, one of the world's best-performing EM currencies in 2026.

### MXN — Mexican Peso
The Mexican peso appreciated **+1.69% vs USD** — one of the week's strongest EM performers — supported by broad USD softness, resilient domestic macro conditions, and elevated oil prices that bolster Mexico's energy revenues. No Banxico meeting this week; the current reference rate is 9.50%.

### NOK — Norwegian Krone
Norges Bank **surprised markets with a 25 bp rate hike to 4.25%**, citing above-target inflation and stronger-than-expected Norwegian house prices (+0.6% MoM in April vs. 0.2% forecast). The hawkish surprise, however, was partially offset by positioning unwinds; USDNOK fell 0.37% on net for the week (NOK +0.37% vs USD). Analysts note a growing NOK-SEK policy divergence.

### SEK — Swedish Krona
The Riksbank **held its policy rate at 1.75%**, citing below-target inflation — Sweden's flash April CPIF fell to **0.8% YoY** (well below the 2% target), with core inflation at 0.0% YoY driven by lower services prices and a VAT cut passing through to food. The Riksbank maintained a vigilant stance but saw no immediate need to tighten. SEK slightly underperformed.

### DKK — Danish Krone
No major domestic releases. Denmark's fixed exchange rate regime pegs DKK tightly to EUR; USDDKK was little changed at 6.3610 (DKK +0.06% vs USD for the week), tracking EUR/ECB dynamics.

### PLN — Polish Zloty
No major Polish economic releases this week. The National Bank of Poland (NBP) last set its reference rate at 5.75% and is in monitoring mode. The PLN tracked EUR/USD moves broadly, trading around 3.92 vs USD.

### HUF — Hungarian Forint
No major releases. The BUX index declined 0.56% on May 7 to 135,139, reflecting broader EM caution and domestic fiscal uncertainties. The HUF was the week's weakest performer among the covered basket (~−0.50% vs USD).

### SGD — Singapore Dollar
No MAS policy meeting this week (MAS meets semi-annually, next review in October). SGD gained approximately +0.95% vs USD, supported by strong regional trade flows, China's better-than-expected PMI data, and an improving risk environment. The STI closed at 4,922 on May 8.

### ILS — Israeli New Shekel
The ILS extended its remarkable appreciation trend, with USDILS closing at **2.8997** — below the psychologically important **3.00 level** (first breached on April 28, 1995 levels), a gain of ~+1.4% for the week vs USD. Israel's economy has demonstrated surprising resilience: the IMF projects GDP growth of 4.8% in 2026, driven by pent-up consumption and investment recovery. S&P revised Israel's outlook to stable.
