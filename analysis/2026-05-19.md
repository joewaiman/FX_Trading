## Per-Currency Detail

### GBP (6/9 — Mild Bull BUY)
BoE voted 8-1 to hold at 3.75% in April, with one member already voting for a 25bp hike. CPI sits at 3.3% YoY (vs 2% target) and JPMorgan expects a hike in June — energy pass-through from the Middle East conflict is pushing inflation higher. Both Manufacturing (53.7) and Services (52.7) PMIs are in expansion. Retail sales +0.7% MoM beat expectations. The only drag is a widening trade deficit and unemployment at 5.0% (softening but not alarming). GBP is the most compelling buy across the major basket — hawkish central bank, above-target CPI, and strong activity data.

### JPY (6/9 — Mild Bull BUY)
The BoJ is the key story: the April 28 meeting ended 6-3 in favour of holding, with 3 dissenters wanting an immediate hike to 1.0%. The BoJ upgraded its FY2026 inflation forecast to 2.8% from 1.9% (citing energy/commodity price pressures from the Middle East) and June is now the consensus for the next hike. Oxford Economics sees normalization resuming in July; Bloomberg forecasts 2% by end-2027. Manufacturing PMI at 55.1 is the strongest in the basket. CPI at 1.5% is currently below target, but the BoJ is forward-looking — when the central bank is hiking, buy the currency regardless of the current CPI print.

### USD (5/9 — Tentative BUY)
Fed has held for 3 consecutive meetings at 3.75%. CPI at 3.8% YoY is well above the 2% target and is "going up instead of down" per the Chicago Fed's Goolsbee — eliminating the case for cuts. JPMorgan forecasts no cuts in 2026 and a hike in Q3 2027. Manufacturing PMI 54.5 is strong. The drag: GDP growth at 2% QoQ (stall speed given tariff uncertainty), services PMI only 51, and the persistent trade deficit (-$60.3B). A new Fed Chair takes over as Powell's term expired May 15 — adds policy uncertainty but does not change the underlying data.

### AUD (5/9 — Tentative BUY)
The RBA is the most hawkish major central bank in the basket: 3 consecutive hikes in 2026, latest to 4.35% in May, fully unwinding the 2025 easing cycle. CPI at 4.6% is well above the 2–3% target band. Employment is solid at 4.3%. CBA forecasts hold for the rest of 2026; Westpac forecasts 2 further hikes to 4.85%. The drag: trade balance moved to deficit (-AUD 1.8B), PMIs only marginally expansionary, and GDP data is from Q4 2025. AUD is a carry currency being supported by active tightening.

### EUR (4/9 — Tentative SELL)
The ECB is stuck. CPI at 3.0% (above the 2% target) means the ECB cannot cut; weak Services PMI at 47.6 (contraction) and Q1 GDP at 0.1% QoQ means it cannot hike. Retail sales -0.1% and unemployment above 6.2% confirm demand weakness. Investors have fully priced out cuts for 2026 and the first hike is pencilled in for 2027 — the ECB is on hold with no near-term catalyst. The trade surplus gives structural support, but the weak domestic economy is a headwind.

### CAD (4/9 — Tentative SELL)
CAD has the worst employment picture in the basket: unemployment at 6.9% is well above the 5.5% threshold, and GDP went negative (-0.2% QoQ in Q4 2025). The BoC cut 9 times from a 5% peak to 2.25% and has paused — but with growth negative and employment deteriorating, further cuts cannot be ruled out. Manufacturing PMI at 53.3 provides some offsetting strength, and CPI at 2.4% gives cover to hold. But the macro picture points toward CAD weakness, not strength.

### NZD (4/9 — Tentative SELL)
RBNZ paused its easing cycle at 2.25%, with the next meeting May 27. CPI at 3.1% is marginally above the 1–3% target band, keeping the RBNZ on hold, but weak PMIs (Manufacturing 50.5, Services 48.9) and only 0.2% QoQ GDP growth (Dec/25) leave the economic outlook tepid. Unemployment at 5.3% is neutral but softening. The trade surplus (NZD 698M) offers some support. Overall, NZD has no near-term catalyst — slight bearish lean due to poor growth trajectory.

### CHF (4/9 — Tentative SELL)
CHF is at the structural bear end of the rate spectrum with the SNB at 0% (floor) and CPI at just 0.6% — within the 0–2% band but close to the lower bound. No hike is expected until H2 2027. The SNB is more likely to intervene in FX markets to weaken CHF than to raise rates. PMIs are actually strong (Manufacturing 54.5, Services 54.8), unemployment is tight (3.0%), and the trade surplus is positive. But the rate story dominates: when the central bank is at zero with no hiking intention for 18+ months, the currency has a structural headwind.

## Macro Risk Note — Middle East Conflict

A consistent thread across all currencies: higher energy prices from the Middle East conflict are lifting CPI across all majors and changing central bank calculus. This is the primary global macro risk for H1 2026. It pushes CPI higher, delays rate cuts, and may force hikes in currencies that would otherwise be on hold. Monitor crude oil — if Brent breaks above $90 sustainably, this inflates the hawks further and may push more central banks toward hike territory.
