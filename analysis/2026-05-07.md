# FX Fundamental Analysis — 2026-05-07

## Macro Backdrop

The dominant theme is the **Iran-related conflict** (ongoing since late 2025) driving energy prices sharply higher, re-accelerating inflation globally, and forcing central banks that had been cutting to pause or pivot hawkish. This creates a **stagflation-lite** environment in Europe while commodity-linked economies benefit from export revenues.

## Per-Currency Detail

### AUD — Strong Bull
RBA delivered its third consecutive 25bp hike to 4.35% on 6 May 2026 in a split 8-1 decision. CPI at 4.6% sits 160bp above the upper band, with the Board noting upside inflation risks from energy prices. Unemployment is healthy at 4.3% (within target band) and GDP expanded at 3.2% annualised in Q4 2025. The only negatives are marginal PMIs (51.3/50.7) and an unusual trade deficit (–A$1.84B) which likely reflects Iran-war energy import costs. Westpac is calling two more hikes to 4.85%; CBA expects a hold. AUD is the highest-carry and most hawkish G8 currency by a meaningful margin.

### USD — Strong Bull
The US economy remains the strongest in G8: GDP +2.7% YoY, ISM Manufacturing 54.5, and retail sales +1.7% MoM in March (likely tariff-related front-loading). CPI at 3.3% keeps the Fed on hold with zero expectation of cuts in 2026. The key caveat: the FOMC majority hinted the next move is a cut (dissenters objected), and Powell's term expired 15 May creating leadership uncertainty. The trade deficit (–$60.3B) is a persistent structural drag. On fundamentals USD is a solid BUY, but with less conviction than AUD.

### CAD — Conditional Bull
BoC officially "done with rate cuts" and signalling hikes for H2 2026 (September base case). CPI is marginally above target at 2.4%, and manufacturing PMI is solid at 53.3. However, the contradiction is stark: unemployment at 6.7% (above the bearish threshold) and GDP contracted –0.2% QoQ. The hike thesis depends on oil-driven inflation persisting. If the Iran conflict de-escalates and oil falls, the BoC will likely not follow through — making CAD a conditional, rather than high-conviction, long.

### JPY — Mild-Strong Bull (carry unwind thesis)
The BoJ is unique in G8: actively normalising rates in a world where others are on hold. At its April meeting the BoJ kept rates at 0.75% but raised its inflation forecast dramatically to 2.8% (from 1.9%) due to the Iran war energy impact. Next hike expected October 2026, potentially reaching 1.0% by end of year. Current CPI at 1.5% is still below the 2% target, but the forward trajectory has shifted sharply higher. Japan's manufacturing PMI at 55.1 is the highest in G8. As the rate differential between JPY and other G8 currencies narrows, carry trade unwinds support JPY appreciation — particularly against USD (eventual cuts) and CHF.

### GBP — Mild Bull
GBP presents a mixed but slightly positive picture. Both PMIs are in solid expansion (manufacturing 53.7, services 52.7), CPI at 3.3% is well above target, and retail sales beat at +0.7% MoM. The BoE held 8-1 with one member voting for a hike, and Governor Bailey warned that higher rates are "unavoidable" if energy-driven inflation persists. The key drag is GDP, which barely grew at 0.4% annualised. GBP is best expressed as a relative long against EUR (where the economy is clearly weaker) rather than against high-carry currencies like AUD or USD.

### CHF — Hold/Avoid as Long, Prime Short Vehicle
The Swiss economy is operationally healthy: PMIs at 54.5/54.8 (strongest services PMI in G8) and a trade surplus of CHF2.74B. However, for FX purposes CHF is the lowest-carry G8 currency (0% rate) with CPI at just 0.6%, dangerously close to deflation. The SNB has explicitly stated it does not want to go negative and will not hike until mid-2027. CHF earns its place as the primary funding/short currency in carry pairs. The strong PMIs provide some safe-haven support — confirm shorts with technical analysis before entry.

### NZD — Hold/Avoid
The red flag is services PMI at 46.0 — the deepest contraction reading in the G8 universe this cycle. GDP grew only 0.8% annualised in Q4 and unemployment is softening at 5.3%. The RBNZ held OCR at 2.25% in April and Capital Economics is calling a cut to 2.0% by mid-2026. CPI at 3.1% is only marginally above the 3.0% upper band — enough to prevent an immediate cut but not enough to justify hikes. NZD is best shorted relative to AUD given the clear hiking divergence and the structural weakness in the services sector.

### EUR — Conflicted
The EUR faces a classic stagflation dilemma. The ECB is expected to hike in June 2026 (markets fully pricing a 25bp increase) because inflation has re-accelerated to 3.0% due to the Iran war energy shock. But the underlying economy is genuinely weak: services PMI 47.6 (contraction), retail sales –0.1%, GDP barely growing at 0.4% annualised, and unemployment at 6.2%. The hike will be inflation-driven, not growth-driven, and risks tightening into a slowing economy. EUR score is propped up by the rate bias and trade surplus. For pair trading, EUR is best used as a short against GBP (relative economic outperformance) or a neutral vs USD.
