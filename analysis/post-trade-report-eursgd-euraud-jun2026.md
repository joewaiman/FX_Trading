## 1. Fundamental Thesis

### Why we were short EUR

EUR was identified as the basket's weakest currency across two consecutive weekly scans:

**W21 (22 May 2026) — EUR scored Strong Bearish (1/9 bullish)**

The ECB was described as being in a **cutting cycle** at the time of the W21 scan. Key bearish signals:
- Services PMI: 46.4 — firmly in contraction (largest sector of the eurozone)
- GDP: near stall (0.1% QoQ / 0.4% annualised)
- Unemployment: 6.2% — above the G10 healthy threshold
- Real wages: −0.54% (negotiated wages 2.46% vs CPI 3.0%)
- Energy inflation: 10.8% YoY — above target but supply-driven, not demand-driven

EUR was the clearest bearish fundamental candidate in the basket. W21 commentary: *"EUR/USD drifted lower to 1.1594 but remains historically elevated — room to fall further on fundamentals."*

**W22 (31 May 2026) — EUR scored Mild Bearish (3🟢 vs 4🔴)**

The score weakened slightly from Strong Bearish to Mild Bearish because the ECB rate bias had shifted — a **25bp hike was now expected for June 11**. The W22 scorecard recorded this as 🟢 for rate bias. However, the trade thesis was maintained because:
- Services PMI remained in contraction (46.4)
- Retail sales still negative (−0.1% MoM)
- Real wages still negative (−0.54%)
- GDP still near-stall (0.1% QoQ)

W22 commentary: *"The ECB is hiking into weakness — a stagflationary dynamic — which historically does not sustain currency strength."*

⚠️ **Hindsight note:** The rate bias flip from 🔴 (cutting) in W21 to 🟢 (hiking) in W22 was a significant fundamental change that reduced EUR short conviction. The overall score moved from 1/9 to 3/10 net bullish vs 4/10 bearish. The trade continued regardless.

## 4. What Went Wrong

### Primary cause: ECB rate hike repricing

The direct cause of both stopouts was a single macro event: **Eurozone CPI for May 2026 printed at 3.2%** (highest since September 2023), which pushed market pricing for an ECB rate hike on June 11 to **90% probability**. EUR strengthened against both SGD and AUD.

This was a **fundamental thesis collision** — we were short EUR based on weak underlying data (services PMI, GDP, retail), but the market repriced EUR based on the inflation print and the imminent ECB hike. Both signals were present in the data; the question was which would dominate the market in the short term.

### Contributing factors

**1. The ECB rate bias had already changed before entry**

By W22, the fundamental scorecard had already flipped the ECB rate bias from 🔴 (cutting) to 🟢 (hiking). EUR moved from Strong Bearish (1/9, W21) to Mild Bearish/Conflicted (3/10 bullish vs 4/10 bearish, W22). A 3/10 vs 4/10 split is close to Neutral — the conviction for a short EUR trade had materially weakened before we entered.

The trades were entered on the strength of a thesis that was valid in W21 but had partially expired by W22. We continued because the underlying economic data was still weak, but the rate catalyst (the thing that moves FX in the near term) had flipped.

**2. No formal macro calendar check at entry**

There was no blocking rule in the entry confirmation process at the time. The June 11 ECB decision was mentioned in the trade notes (*"Consider reducing/exiting before June 11 if TP1 not reached"*) but this was advisory, not a hard gate. Under the new rules now in place, the ECB decision 10 days away would have triggered a **24–72h warning at a minimum**, flagging halved position size and widened SLs — and would have blocked re-entry entirely within 24h.

**3. EUR/SGD stop loss was too tight**

The original provisional trade setup (31 May analysis) had a 50-pip SL above the Daily SMA20. The actual entry used a 24-pip SL above the entry price — half of what was planned and placed at the entry zone rather than a structural level.

The structural invalidation level — the price above which the bearish channel thesis would be broken — was the **Weekly SMA50 (~1.4974)**. A structurally placed SL would have been ~1.498–1.499, approximately 35 pips from entry. Price reached 1.4906 on the stopout day and has not extended beyond 1.491 since — a 35-pip stop would have survived.

**4. EUR/AUD exit rule was more conservative than the hard SL**

The hard SL for EUR/AUD was 1.62855 (55 pips, appropriate for a daily setup). However, the active exit rule was "exit immediately if 4H closes above 1.62485" — which triggered at approximately 1.625, 33 pips from entry. The hard SL was also eventually hit intraday (high 1.6296) but only by 10 pips.

The exit rule was correct in principle (preserving capital) but created a de facto stop at 33 pips instead of 55 pips — on a daily setup where 55 pips was specifically calculated as the structural level. The rule was appropriate for normal conditions but the EUR strength move was macro-driven, not a structural breakdown of the trade thesis.

**5. Correlation risk was managed but not eliminated**

Both trades were EUR short, entered on the same day. Half-sizing was applied to EUR/AUD specifically due to this correlation. The correlation note was documented. This was the right approach — the loss was contained to −44 pips combined rather than doubled from a full-size EUR/AUD position.

## 5. What Was Done Well

- **EUR/AUD was half-sized** from the start due to the Weekly RSI oversold warning and correlation flag. This was the correct risk management decision.
- **The Weekly RSI oversold condition on EUR/AUD (27.93) was flagged multiple times** and was ultimately what drove the bounce that stopped us out. The risk was identified — the entry was taken anyway, which was the judgment call.
- **The ECB event was not ignored** — it was documented as a risk in every analysis from June 1 onwards. The issue was it wasn't a hard blocking condition.
- **EUR/SGD** performed its technical role correctly — price bounced into the Daily SMA resistance cluster, produced a bearish engulfing, and we entered the correct way. The trade was structurally sound. The exit was caused by macro, not a technical failure.
- **EUR/AUD** had the strongest possible entry signal — a 9.5× wick:body bearish pin bar, Grade A quality, at the 4H SMA cluster with all 3 timeframes aligned. The entry was textbook.

**EUR/SGD:** The ECB decision was **10 days away** on June 1 (entry date). Under the new rules:

- However, the June 11 event would have appeared explicitly in the Stage C output, prompting a judgment call on whether to enter
- The SL would have been placed at ~1.498 (Weekly SMA50) instead of 1.49005 — a 36-pip stop vs 24 pips
- At 36 pips with today's high at 1.4906, the trade would **still be open** (price hasn't reached 1.498)

**EUR/AUD:** Same 10-day horizon. Under the new rules:
- Hard SL was already at an appropriate structural level (55 pips)
- The exit rule ("exit if 4H closes above 1.62485") would still have been active
- The ECB flag in the Stage C output might have prompted waiting for a clearer setup or smaller size
- EUR/AUD fundamental thesis was already borderline at entry (W RSI 27.93, conflicted fundamentals) — under W23 scoring, AUD is now Neutral, which would make this trade no longer viable
