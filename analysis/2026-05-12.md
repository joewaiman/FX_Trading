## Per-Currency Narrative

### USD
The Federal Reserve held rates at 3.75% at the April 29 FOMC meeting in an unusually close 8-4 vote — the closest dissent since 1992, with four members preferring an immediate hike. PCE inflation re-accelerated to 3.5% in March (from 2.8% in February), and April CPI printed 3.8%, both well above the 2% target. GDP grew 2.0% annualized in Q1, unemployment held at 4.3%, and retail sales surged +1.7% MoM in March. The sole structural negative is the persistent trade deficit (−$60.3B). Markets are pricing zero probability of a 2026 rate cut and approximately 47% odds of a hike before mid-2027. USD is the joint-highest conviction long alongside GBP.

### GBP
The Bank of England held Bank Rate at 3.75% with an 8-1 vote on April 30, with one member voting for 4%. CPI was 3.3% in March, above the 2% target, and the Middle East oil shock is expected to keep inflation elevated. Both PMI sectors remain in expansion (manufacturing 53.7, services 52.7), retail sales are solid (+0.7% in March), and the broader economic picture is constructive despite softening unemployment (4.9%). GDP data from Q4 2025 is stale — Q1 2026 GDP data expected mid-May could refresh this reading. Markets are pricing potential BoE hikes later in 2026. GBP ties with USD for the top fundamental ranking.

### JPY
The highest-impact forward-looking story in the basket. Bloomberg reported on May 12 that the BoJ is signaling a rate hike as early as June, with board members citing upside inflation risks from energy prices and second-round wage effects. The 2026 spring wage round produced average increases above 5% for the third consecutive year. The BoJ has raised its FY2026 core CPI forecast to 2.8% from 1.9%. Current CPI at 1.5% remains below the 2% target — but the BoJ is forward-looking, and rate expectations (🟢 ×2) override the lagging CPI print under the fundamental rule. Manufacturing PMI of 55.1 is the highest in the basket. If the BoJ hikes in June or July, expect a sharp JPY carry unwind: USD/JPY and EUR/JPY both carry significant short-JPY positioning that would unwind rapidly.

### AUD
The RBA raised the cash rate 25bp to 4.35% at its May 2026 meeting, the third consecutive hike in 2026, fully unwinding the prior easing cycle. Governor Bullock cited the oil shock as a complicating factor, adding to already-elevated domestic inflation (CPI 4.6% vs 2.0–3.0% target band). GDP is healthy at 2.6% YoY, unemployment is comfortably within the sweet spot at 4.3%, and retail spending is growing strongly (+5.7% YoY). The trade balance turned negative (−A$1.8B) and PMIs are only marginally above 50, which are the main drags on conviction. CBA expects the RBA to pause for the remainder of 2026, so rate expectations may plateau — but the hiking cycle is complete enough to keep AUD well-supported.

### CHF
The SNB held rates at 0% in March 2026 and is expected to remain on hold through year-end; the first hike is not projected until H2 2027. Despite neutral rate policy, CHF scores well on economic fundamentals: both PMI sectors are strong (manufacturing 54.5, services 54.8 — highest services reading in the basket), unemployment is tight at 3.0%, and the trade surplus of CHF2.7B is supportive. CPI at 0.6% is well inside the 0–2% target band, removing any pressure to hike. CHF's safe-haven status provides additional support given ongoing Middle East tensions. The carry trade disadvantage vs USD (−375bp) and GBP (−375bp) limits conviction for a standalone long; CHF is best as a defensive allocation or paired against higher-yielding weak currencies (e.g., CHF/CAD).

### NZD
The RBNZ held the OCR at 2.25% in April 2026 with the next decision on May 27. CPI at 3.1% has crossed above the 3.0% upper bound of the 1–3% target band, creating pressure for a hike; markets are pricing a 25% chance of action at the May meeting and at least two 25bp hikes by year-end. Manufacturing PMI is solid (53.2) and the trade balance is in surplus. However, services PMI has fallen sharply to 46.0 (contraction) and unemployment is elevated at 5.3%. The RBNZ describes its outlook as a "balancing act" — near-term inflation up (Middle East energy) but medium-term inflation outlook uncertain if growth weakens. NZD is a tentative buy with the May 27 decision as the key catalyst; a hike would upgrade NZD toward Strong Bull.

### EUR
EUR presents a fundamental paradox in May 2026. Markets are pricing three 25bp ECB rate hikes in 2026 starting as early as June — driven by CPI at 3.0% above the 2% target — which pushes rate expectations to 🟢, inflating the headline score. But the underlying economy is deteriorating: services PMI fell to 47.6 (contraction), retail sales turned negative (−0.1% MoM), unemployment is 6.2% (above the 5.5% bearish threshold), and GDP grew only 0.4% annualized in Q1 2026. If the ECB hikes into a weakening services sector and falling consumer spending, the risk of a recession rises materially — a medium-term EUR negative that offsets the rate-driven short-term support. EUR is best used as the short side of pairs against USD, GBP, and JPY.

### CAD
The Bank of Canada held rates at 2.25% in April and explicitly adopted a neutral stance: it may cut if US trade restrictions intensify, or hike if oil prices remain elevated. The fundamental picture is the weakest in the basket on labour and growth: Q4 2025 GDP contracted (−0.8% annualized), unemployment is 6.9% — the highest in the basket and well above the 5.5% bearish threshold. Bright spots include a solid manufacturing PMI (53.3), positive retail sales (+0.6%), and a trade surplus (C$1.8B), but these are not sufficient to offset the macro weakness signals. The BoC's next decision is June 10 — if the trade situation deteriorates, a cut is on the table. CAD is the preferred short in North American pairs.
